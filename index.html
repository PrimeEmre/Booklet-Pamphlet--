Information about the 3 levels of branches in Canada and what they stand for?

1. Legislative Branch: It debates the law and passes the new law. It also elects the representatives (people) at the provincial level, such as the federal level, and it is made in the House of Commons because when there is a new law, or a law gets developed, the politicians make a debate about the laws.



2. Executive Branch: Develops and applies laws. When the laws are applied or developed, the governor-general prime minister and his cabinet assign them.


3. Judicial Branch: This branch focuses on the courts of Canada and checks the law to be fair so people can have rights and benefit from it. If the laws are unfair or people can not benefit from them, they make a new law, and they will make a new law that can benefit people, and they can have rights when that law is published to society.

History of Canada



Canada was first explored by Europeans in 1534 when Jacques Cartier arrived. By 1550, Canada started appearing on world maps. In 1610, King Charles II of England began negotiations with the Hudson Bay Company, a firm focused on fur trading. The fur trade involved clothing, hats, beaver pelts, and furs. In 1670, fur trading became an economic priority to strengthen the economy and boost manufacturing.

Canada played a significant role in World War I. As a part of the British Empire, Canada was compelled to enter the war against Germany when Britain did. This alliance required Canada to support Britain, which was part of the Triple Entente, a coalition opposing the Triple Alliance led by Germany.

World War I began in 1914 following the assassination of Archduke Franz Ferdinand of Austria, sparking a complex web of alliances between countries. Much of the combat was trench warfare, where Canada demonstrated solid defensive tactics. German forces eventually surrendered to the Canadian forces by capturing their trenches. One of Canada’s key military leaders, General Sir Arthur Currie, played a vital role in securing victory at battles like Ypres in the trenches.
